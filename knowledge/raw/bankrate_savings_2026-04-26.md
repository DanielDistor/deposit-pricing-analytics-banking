## Rate trends for savings accounts

Savings account rates have been declining in recent years — in response to six Federal Reserve rate cuts in 2024 and 2025. Despite this trend, many competitive annual percentage yields (APYs) still beat inflation, so your money can keep (or even increase) its purchasing power.

However, savings accounts typically earn variable APYs, meaning banks can decide to change them at any time. If your primary goal is to have a savings account that earns the highest rate, it pays to watch that APY over time and make sure it remains competitive.

That said, chasing the absolute highest APY might not always be your top priority, especially if your existing account pays a competitive enough rate and provides other perks you value — such as no fees, unlimited withdrawals or a user-friendly mobile app.

Learn more:
[Savings account rate forecast for 2026](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/savings/savings-money-market-account-rate-forecast/)

It's especially a good time to open a high-yield savings account if:

- You’re not earning any interest — or you’re earning a low APY — in your current bank account.
- You don’t have an emergency fund, or you have your emergency fund commingled with your checking account.
- You're saving for a major medium- or short-term goal, such as a vacation, wedding or home down payment.

## How to choose a high-yield savings account

When shopping around for the right account, take a look at Bankrate’s [expert reviews of popular banks](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/reviews/), many of which offer competitive rates. Also consider these important features when [choosing the best high-yield savings account](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/how-to-choose-the-best-high-yield-savings-account/):

1. ### A competitive yield





Your savings will grow more quickly when you’re earning higher interest. These days, competitive accounts are earning up to around eight times the national average of 0.59%. The difference between a high APY and a mediocre one could add up to hundreds, if not thousands, in interest over time.

2. ### No monthly service fee





Rather than letting a monthly service fee eat into your savings, find an account that won’t charge this pesky fee. None of the options on Bankrate’s list charge such a fee.

3. ### Access to your money when you need it





Being able to withdraw your money easily is important, especially in an emergency. You may be able to take money out of your savings account at an ATM, using a peer-to-peer money transfer app or through an ACH transfer. You may also want to seek banks that allow unlimited withdrawals each month, rather than restricting you to six.

## Ways to use a high-yield savings account

A high-yield savings account provides easy access to your cash, making it a good place to earn some interest while you [build up an emergency fund](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/savings/starting-an-emergency-fund/) or save for goals such as a wedding or [down payment on a house](https://www.bankrate.com/mortgages/how-to-save-for-a-down-payment/). While you’re adding to your savings, consider using the interest you earn to pay for discretionary expenses such as a streaming service or a gym membership.

If you’re saving for multiple goals in a single account, it can pay to choose an account that allows you to separate the funds into categories. This way, it’s easy to track how much you have saved for each goal. For instance, [Ally Bank](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/reviews/ally-bank/) lets you create up to 30 savings "buckets."

One way to grow your nest egg is by setting up recurring [automatic transfers](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/savings/grow-your-savings-with-automatic-transfers/). Moving a set amount from your checking to your savings every payday helps ensure you’ll save consistently and reach your goals.

## Frequently asked questions about savings accounts

- ### Is my money safe in a high-yield savings account?

Caret Down Icon






High-yield savings accounts are safe if they’re offered from a bank that’s insured by the [Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. (FDIC)](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/fdic-insurance/) or a credit union that’s a member of the [National Credit Union Administration (NCUA)](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/ncua-how-your-savings-at-credit-unions-are-insured-by-the-government/). This way, your money won’t be lost if the financial institution fails.



At both banks and credit unions, federal deposit insurance limits are $250,000 per depositor, per insured institution, per ownership category.

- ### What should I do if I am unable to open a high-yield savings account?

Caret Down Icon






If you’re unsuccessful in opening a new account, you might want to go to ChexSystems’ website and request a report to see whether your banking history is the reason you were denied the account.



[ChexSystems](https://www.bankrate.com/banking/what-is-chexsystems/) is a national specialty consumer reporting agency that keeps track of some of your banking history. Things that can appear on a ChexSystems report include your check-cashing history, any suspected fraudulent activity and a list of closed accounts.

- ### Should I get a CD instead of a high-yield savings account?

Caret Down Icon






CDs generally earn a fixed APY for a set period of time. In exchange for the guaranteed return, you agree to leave your money locked away for the chosen term. With high-yield savings accounts, you deal with variable APYs in exchange for being able to withdraw your money at any time. This makes a high-yield savings account a better place for your emergency fund or other money you may need in the near term.
